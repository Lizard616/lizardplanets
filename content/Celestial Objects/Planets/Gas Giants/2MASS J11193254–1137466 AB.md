**2MASS J11193254–1137466 AB** (often shortened to **2MASS J1119–1137 AB**) is a [planetary mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_mass_object) binary located 86±23 [light-years](/uncategorized/light-year/) from the [Earth](/celestial-objects/planets/subearths/earth/) in the [constellation Crater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation_Crater_(crater)). The components of 2MASS J1119–1137 are each roughly four [Jupiter masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter_masses). The planetary-mass objects are probably a part of the [TW Hydrae association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TW_Hydrae_association) which has an age of approximately 10 million years. The planetary-mass objects are candidate [rogue planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rogue_(planet)).

## Overview
The object was found by a team of scientists from Canada, the United States and Chile during a search for unusually red [brown dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown_dwarf) (such color indicates some notable properties of their atmospheres, e.g. dustiness). The search used data of 3 surveys: [SDSS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SDSS) (visible light data), [2MASS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2MASS) (near-infrared) and [WISE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WISE) (mid-wave infrared). 2MASS J1119–1137 was one of the reddest and, according to the authors, the most interesting object found. Results of the work were published in December 2015.

In April 2016, the first detailed study of the object was published. The investigators conducted its infrared spectroscopy on the telescope [Gemini South](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gemini_South). [Radial velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radial_velocity) and [proper motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_motion) were also calculated. The astronomers determined low [surface gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_gravity) and moderate age of 2MASS J1119–1137.

In November 2016 and March 2017, 2MASS J1119–1137 was imaged by the telescope [Keck II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keck_II) with [adaptive optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptive_optics) technique, which revealed its binarity. The [angular separation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_separation) of components is 0.13788 ± 0.00034 arcseconds (which corresponds to linear [projected separation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projected_separation) 3.6 ± 0.9 [AU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AU). Their [stellar magnitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_magnitude) are roughly equal. Total mass of the system is estimated as 7.4+2.5 −1.9 [Jupiter masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter_masses). Their total [bolometric luminosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolometric_luminosity) is approximately 0.00004 [solar units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_luminosity). The estimated orbital period is 90+80 −50 years. One of the components<sup>[*[which?](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia_avoid_weasel_words)*]</sup> of the binary is rotating rapidly, having a period of 3.02+0.04 −0.03 hours while the typical rotation period for young brown dwarfs is 10 hours.

## Candidate exomoon
In August 2021, researchers reported signs of a [habitable zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitable_zone) 1.7 [<var>R</var><sub>🜨</sub>](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_radius) [exomoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exomoon) (a moon orbiting a planetary-mass object outside our solar system) transiting one of the components in 2MASS J1119–1137. A possible single [transit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transit_method) of the moon candidate was detected in archival [Spitzer Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope) data. The study determined that the detected event might have been caused by variability (clouds/weather) in the host planet's atmosphere, but that an exomoon was a better fit to the observed data suggesting that the detection was most likely caused by an exomoon transit.
