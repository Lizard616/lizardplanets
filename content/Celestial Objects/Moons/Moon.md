## Moon/Exomoon
An **exomoon** or **extrasolar moon** is a [natural satellite](/celestial-objects/moons/natural-satellite/) that orbits an [exoplanet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoplanet) or other non-stellar [extrasolar body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object_categories_20by_20location).Exomoons are difficult to detect and confirm using current techniques, and to date there have been no confirmed exomoon detections. However, observations from missions such as [Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler_(spacecraft)) have observed a number of candidates. Two potential exomoons that may orbit [rogue planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rogue_(planet)) have also been detected by [microlensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microlensing). In September 2019, astronomers reported that the observed dimmings of [Tabby's Star](/celestial-objects/stars/tabby-s-star/) may have been produced by fragments resulting from the [disruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disrupted_(planet)) of an [orphaned exomoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidally_detached_exomoon). Some exomoons may be potential [habitats for extraterrestrial life](/celestial-objects/moons/natural-satellite-habitability/).

## Definition and designation
Although traditional usage implies [moons](/celestial-objects/moons/natural-satellite/) orbit a [planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet), the discovery of [brown dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brown_dwarf) with [planet-sized satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_mass_(moon)) blurs the distinction between planets and moons, due to the low mass of brown dwarfs. This confusion is resolved by the [International Astronomical Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union) (IAU) declaration that "Objects with true masses below the limiting mass for thermonuclear fusion of deuterium that orbit stars, brown dwarfs or stellar remnants and that have a mass ratio with the central object below the [L4/L5 instability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L4/L5_instability) (M/M<sub>central</sub> < 2/(25+√621) are planets."

The IAU definition does not address the naming convention for the satellites of free-floating objects that are less massive than brown dwarfs and below the deuterium limit (the objects are typically referred to as free-floating planets, [rogue planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rogue_(planet)), low-mass brown dwarfs or isolated planetary-mass objects). The satellites of these objects are typically referred to as exomoons in the literature.

Exomoons take their designation from that of their [parent body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_astronomy) plus a capital [Roman numeral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_numeral); thus, [Kepler-1625b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-1625b) orbits [Kepler-1625](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-1625) (synonymous with Kepler-1625a) and is itself orbited by [Kepler-1625b I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-1625b_I) (no Kepler-1625b II is known, nor is I known to have a [submoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submoon).

## Characteristics
Characteristics of any extrasolar satellite are likely to vary, as do the Solar System's [moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_solar_system_objects_in_hydrostatic_equilibrium_round_20satellites). For extrasolar giant planets orbiting within their stellar [habitable zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitable_zone), there is the prospect that [terrestrial planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrestrial_(planet))-sized satellite may be capable of supporting life.<sup>[*[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia_please_clarify)*]</sup>

In August 2019, astronomers reported that an exomoon in the WASP-49b exoplanet system may be volcanically active.

## Orbital inclination
For impact-generated moons of [terrestrial planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrestrial_(planet)) not too far from their star, with a large planet–moon distance, it is expected that the orbital planes of moons will tend to be aligned with the planet's orbit around the star due to tides from the star, but if the planet–moon distance is small it may be inclined. For [gas giants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_giant), the orbits of moons will tend to be aligned with the giant planet's equator because these formed in circumplanetary disks.

## Lack of moons around planets close to their stars
Planets close to their stars on circular orbits will tend to despin and become [tidally locked](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidally_locked). As the planet's rotation slows down the radius of a [synchronous orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synchronous_orbit) of the planet moves outwards from the planet. For planets tidally locked to their stars, the distance from the planet at which the moon will be in a synchronous orbit around the planet is outside the [Hill sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hill_sphere) of the planet. The Hill sphere of the planet is the region where its gravity dominates that of the star so it can hold on to its moons. Moons inside the synchronous orbit radius of a planet will spiral into the planet. Therefore, if the synchronous orbit is outside the Hill sphere, then all moons will spiral into the planet. If the synchronous orbit is not [three-body stable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_body_problem) then moons outside this radius will escape orbit before they reach the synchronous orbit.

A study on tidal-induced migration offered a feasible explanation for this lack of exomoons. It showed the physical evolution of host planets (i.e. interior structure and size) plays a major role in their final fate: synchronous orbits can become transient states and moons are prone to be stalled in semi-asymptotic semimajor axes, or even ejected from the system, where other effects can appear. In turn, this would have a great impact on the detection of extrasolar satellites.

## Detection methods
The existence of exomoons around many [exoplanets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoplanet) is theorized. Despite the great successes of planet hunters with [Doppler spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_spectroscopy) of the host star, exomoons cannot be found with this technique. This is because the resultant shifted stellar spectra due to the presence of a planet plus additional satellites would behave identically to a single point-mass moving in orbit of the host star. In recognition of this, there have been several other methods proposed for detecting exomoons, including:

- [Direct imaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_imaging)
- [Microlensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microlensing)
- [Pulsar timing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsar_timing)
- [Transit timing effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transit_timing_effects)
- [Transit method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transit_method)

### Direct imaging
Direct imaging of an exoplanet is extremely challenging due to the large difference in brightness between the star and exoplanet as well as the small size and irradiance of the planet. These problems are greater for exomoons in most cases. However, it has been theorized that tidally heated exomoons could shine as brightly as some exoplanets. [Tidal forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_forces) can heat up an exomoon because energy is dissipated by differential forces on it. [Io](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Io_(moon)), a tidally heated moon orbiting [Jupiter](/celestial-objects/planets/gas-giants/jupiter/), has volcanoes powered by tidal forces. If a tidally heated exomoon is sufficiently tidally heated and is distant enough from its star for the moon's light not to be drowned out, it would be possible for a telescope such as the [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope) to image it.

### Doppler spectroscopy of host planet
Doppler spectroscopy is an indirect detection method that measures the velocity shift and resulting stellar spectrum shift associated with an orbiting planet. This method is also known as the Radial Velocity method. It is most successful for main sequence stars. The spectra of exoplanets have been successfully partially retrieved for several cases, including [HD 189733 b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HD_189733_b) and [HD 209458 b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HD_209458_b). The quality of the retrieved spectra is significantly more affected by noise than the stellar spectrum. As a result, the [spectral resolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_resolution), and number of retrieved spectral features, is much lower than the level required to perform Doppler spectroscopy of the exoplanet.

### Radio wave emissions from the host planet's magnetosphere
During its orbit, [Io](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Io_(moon))'s ionosphere interacts with [Jupiter](/celestial-objects/planets/gas-giants/jupiter/)'s [magnetosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere), to create a frictional current that causes radio wave emissions. These are called "Io-controlled decametric emissions" and the researchers believe finding similar emissions near known exoplanets could be key to predicting where other moons exist.

### Microlensing
In 2002, Cheongho Han & Wonyong Han proposed [microlensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microlensing) be used to detect exomoons. The authors found detecting satellite signals in lensing light curves will be very difficult because the signals are seriously smeared out by the severe finite-source effect even for events involved with source stars with small angular radii.

### Pulsar timing
In 2008, Lewis, [Sackett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sackett), and Mardling of the [Monash University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monash_University), Australia, proposed using [pulsar timing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methods_of_detecting_extrasolar_planets_pulsar_20timing) to detect the moons of [pulsar planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsar_planets). The authors applied their method to the case of [PSR B1620-26 b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSR_B1620-26_b) and found that a stable moon orbiting this planet could be detected, if the moon had a separation of about one-fiftieth of that of the orbit of the planet around the pulsar and a mass ratio to the planet of 5% or larger.

### Transit timing effects
In 2007, physicists A. Simon, K. Szatmáry, and Gy. M. Szabó published a research note titled 'Determination of the size, mass, and density of "exomoons" from photometric transit timing variations'.

In 2009, David Kipping published a paper outlining how by combining multiple observations of variations in the time of mid-transit (TTV, caused by the planet leading or trailing the planet–moon system's [barycenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycenter) when the pair are oriented roughly perpendicular to the line of sight) with variations of the transit duration (TDV, caused by the planet moving along the direction path of transit relative to the planet–moon system's barycenter when the moon–planet axis lies roughly along the line of sight) a unique exomoon signature is produced. Furthermore, the work demonstrated how both the mass of the exomoon and its orbital distance from the planet could be determined using the two effects.

In a later study, Kipping concluded that [habitable zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitable_zone) exomoons could be detected by the [Kepler Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler_Space_Telescope) using the TTV and TDV effects.

### Transit method (star-planet-moon systems)
When an exoplanet passes in front of the host star, a small dip in the light received from the star may be observed. The transit method is currently the most successful and responsive method for detecting exoplanets. This effect, also known as occultation, is proportional to the square of the planet's radius. If a planet and a moon pass in front of a host star, both objects should produce a dip in the observed light. A planet–moon eclipse may also occur during the transit, but such events have an inherently low probability.

### Transit method (planet-moon systems)
If the host planet is directly imaged, then transits of an exomoon may be observable. When an exomoon passes in front of the host planet, a small dip in the light received from the directly-imaged planet may be detected. Exomoons of directly imaged exoplanets and free-floating planets are predicted to have a high transit probability and occurrence rate. Moons as small as [Io](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Io_(moon)) or [Titan](/celestial-objects/moons/titan-moon/) should be detectable with the [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope) using this method, but this search method requires a substantial amount of observation time.

### Orbital sampling effects
If a glass bottle is held up to the light it is easier to see through the middle of the glass than it is near the edges. Similarly, a sequence of samples of a moon's position will be more bunched up at the edges of the moon's orbit of a planet than in the middle. If a moon orbits a planet that [transits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transit_method) its star then the moon will also transit the star and this bunching up at the edges may be detectable in the transit light curves if a sufficient number of measurements are made. The larger the star the greater the number of measurements needed to create observable bunching. The [Kepler telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler_telescope) data may contain enough data to detect moons around red dwarfs using orbital sampling effects but won't have enough data for Sun-like stars.

### Indirect detection around white dwarfs
The atmosphere of [white dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf) can be polluted with [metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallicity) and in a few cases, the white dwarfs are surrounded by a [debris disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debris_disk). Usually, this pollution is caused by [asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid) or [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet), but [tidally disrupted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_force) exomoons were also proposed in the past as a source of white dwarf pollution. In 2021 Klein and collaborators discovered that the white dwarfs GD 378 and GALEXJ2339 had an unusually high pollution with [beryllium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beryllium). The researchers conclude that [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen), [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon) or [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen) atoms must have been subjected to [MeV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MeV) collisions with [protons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton) in order to create this excess of beryllium. In one proposed scenario, the beryllium excess is caused by a tidally disrupted exomoon. In this scenario a moon-forming icy disk exists around a [giant planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_(planet)), which orbits the white dwarf. The strong [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field) of such a giant planet accelerates [stellar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_wind) particles, such as protons, and directs them into the disk. The accelerated proton collides with [water ice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice) in the disk, creating elements like beryllium, [boron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boron), and [lithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium) in a [spallation reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray_spallation). These three elements are relatively rare in the universe as they are destroyed in the process of stellar fusion. A [moonlet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlet) forming in this kind of disk would have a higher beryllium, boron and lithium abundance. The study also predicted that the mid-sized moons of [Saturn](/celestial-objects/planets/gas-giants/saturn/), for example, [Mimas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mimas_(moon)), should be enriched in Be, B, and Li.

## Detection projects
There are several missions underway now using some of the methods described above, which will find many more candidate exomoons and be able to confirm or disprove some candidates. [PLATO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PLATO_(spacecraft)), for example, is expected to launch in 2026.

As part of the *Kepler* mission, the [Hunt for Exomoons with Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hunt_for_Exomoons_with_Kepler) (HEK) project was intended to detect exomoons, and generated some of the candidates still discussed today.

## Habitability
Main article: [Habitability of natural satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitability_of_natural_satellites)
impression of a hypothetical Earth-like moon around a Saturn-like exoplanet
The habitability of exomoons has been considered in at least two studies published in peer-reviewed journals. René Heller & Rory Barnes considered stellar and planetary illumination on moons as well as the effect of eclipses on their orbit-averaged surface illumination. They also considered [tidal heating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_heating) as a threat to their habitability. In Sect. 4 in their paper, they introduce a new concept to define the habitable orbits of moons. Referring to the concept of the circumstellar habitable zone for planets, they define an inner border for a moon to be habitable around a certain planet and call it the circumplanetary "habitable edge". Moons closer to their planet than the habitable edge are uninhabitable. In a second study, René Heller then included the effect of eclipses into this concept as well as constraints from a satellite's orbital stability. He found that, depending on a moon's orbital eccentricity, there is a minimum mass for stars to host habitable moons at around 0.2 solar masses.

Taking as an example the smaller [Europa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa_(moon)), at less than 1% the mass of the Earth, Lehmer et al. found if it were to end up near to Earth orbit it would only be able to hold onto its atmosphere for a few million years. However, for any larger, [Ganymede](/celestial-objects/moons/ganymede-moon/)-sized moons venturing into its solar system's habitable zone, an atmosphere and surface water could be retained indefinitely. Models for moon formation suggest the formation of even more massive moons than Ganymede is common around many of the super-Jovian exoplanets.

Earth-sized exoplanets in the habitable zone around [M-dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M-dwarfs) are often [tidally locked](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_locking) to the host star. This has the effect that one hemisphere always faces the star, while the other remains in darkness. An exomoon in an M-dwarf system does not face this challenge, as it is tidally locked to the planet and it would receive light for both hemispheres. Martínez-Rodríguez et al. studied the possibility of exomoons around planets that orbit M-dwarfs in the habitable zone. While they found 33 exoplanets from earlier studies that lie in the habitable zone, only four could host [Moon](/celestial-objects/moons/moon/)- to [Titan](/celestial-objects/moons/titan-moon/)-mass exomoons for timescales longer than 0.8 [Gyr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyr) ([HIP 12961](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HIP_12961) b, [HIP 57050](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HIP_57050) b, [Gliese 876](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gliese_876) b and c). For this mass range the exomoons could probably not hold onto their atmosphere. The researchers increased the mass for the exomoons and found that exomoons with the mass of [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars) around IL Aquarii b and c could be stable on timescales above the [Hubble time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_time). The [CHEOPS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CHEOPS) mission could detect exomoons around the brightest M-dwarfs or [ESPRESSO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ESPRESSO) could detect the [Rossiter–McLaughlin effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rossiter–McLaughlin_effect) caused by the exomoons. Both methods require a transiting exoplanet, which is not the case for these four candidates.

Like an exoplanet, an exomoon can potentially become tidally locked to its primary. However, since the exomoon's primary is an exoplanet, it would continue to rotate relative to its star after becoming tidally locked, and thus would still experience a day/night cycle indefinitely.

The possible exomoon candidate transiting [2MASS J1119-1137AB](/celestial-objects/planets/gas-giants/2mass-j11193254-1137466-ab/) lies in the habitable zone of its host (at least initially until the planet cools), but it is unlikely complex life has formed as the system is only 10 [Myr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myr) old. If confirmed, the exomoon may be similar to primordial earth and characterization of its atmosphere with the [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope) could perhaps place limits on the time scale for the formation of life.

## Moon
A natural satellite is a celestial body that orbits a planet, dwarf planet, or another larger body in space due to the gravitational pull of the object it is orbiting. These natural satellites can vary greatly in size, composition, and geological activity.

Natural satellites are formed through natural processes, often involving the same materials and events that led to the formation of their parent bodies. They can originate from the accumulation of debris around a planet during the solar system's formation, the capture of asteroids or comets by a planet's gravity, or the result of massive impacts that eject material into orbit, which later coalesces into a satellite.

The most well-known example of a natural satellite is Earth's Moon, which has a significant influence on our planet's tides and has been a critical object of study for understanding planetary formation and geophysical processes. Other planets in our solar system, such as Jupiter and Saturn, boast a large number of natural satellites. For instance, Jupiter has 79 known moons, including Ganymede, the largest moon in the solar system, which is even bigger than the planet Mercury.

Natural satellites can exhibit a wide range of surface features and geological activity. Some, like Jupiter's moon Io, are volcanically active, while others, like Saturn's moon Titan, have thick atmospheres and liquid hydrocarbon lakes. These moons are of great interest to scientists as they provide insights into the diversity of celestial bodies and the complex gravitational interactions within our solar system.

Beyond our solar system, exoplanetary systems can also have natural satellites. One fascinating example is Lizard-953-E, an exoplanet located in a distant star system, which has a gaseous moon named Neclite. Neclite is unique among natural satellites because it resembles a miniature gas giant rather than a solid, rocky body. Its thick atmosphere is composed primarily of Water Vapor, with trace amounts of methane giving it a distinctive bluish hue. The presence of Neclite challenges our understanding of moon formation, suggesting that moons can form and maintain stable atmospheres similar to those of gas giants. This discovery has significant implications for the study of planetary systems and the potential for diverse forms of celestial bodies in the universe.
