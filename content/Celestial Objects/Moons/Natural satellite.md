A **natural satellite** is, in the most common usage, an [astronomical body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object) that [orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit) a [planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet), [dwarf planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_(planet)), or [small Solar System body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_Solar_System_body) (or sometimes another natural satellite). Natural satellites are colloquially referred to as **moons**, a derivation from the [Moon](/celestial-objects/moons/moon/) of [Earth](/celestial-objects/planets/subearths/earth/).

In the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System), there are six planetary [satellite systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_system_astronomy) containing 288 known natural satellites altogether. Seven objects commonly considered [dwarf planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_(planet)) by astronomers are also known to have natural satellites: [Orcus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orcus), [Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluto), [Haumea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haumea), [Quaoar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaoar), [Makemake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makemake), [Gonggong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gonggong_(planet)), and [Eris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eris_(planet)). As of January 2022, there are 447 other [minor planets known to have natural satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor_planet_(moon)).

A planet usually has at least around 10,000 times the mass of any natural satellites that orbit it, with a correspondingly much larger diameter. The [Earth–Moon system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth–Moon_system) is a unique exception in the Solar System; at 3,474 kilometres (2,158 miles) across, the Moon is 0.273 times the [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter) of Earth and about 1⁄80 of its mass. The next largest ratios are the [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune)–[Triton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triton_(moon)) system at 0.055 (with a mass ratio of about 1 to 4790), the [Saturn](/celestial-objects/planets/gas-giants/saturn/)–[Titan](/celestial-objects/moons/titan-moon/) system at 0.044 (with the second mass ratio next to the Earth–Moon system, 1 to 4220), the [Jupiter](/celestial-objects/planets/gas-giants/jupiter/)–[Ganymede](/celestial-objects/moons/ganymede-moon/) system at 0.038, and the [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus)–[Titania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titania_(moon)) system at 0.031. For the category of [dwarf planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_(planet)), [Charon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charon_(moon)) has the largest ratio, being 0.52 the diameter and 12.2% the mass of [Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluto).

## Terminology
The first known natural satellite was the [Moon](/celestial-objects/moons/moon/), but it was considered a "planet" until [Copernicus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copernicus)' introduction of *[De revolutionibus orbium coelestium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_revolutionibus_orbium_coelestium)* in 1543. Until the discovery of the [Galilean satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_satellites) in 1610 there was no opportunity for referring to such objects as a class. [Galileo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo) chose to refer to his discoveries as *Planetæ* ("planets"), but later discoverers chose other terms to distinguish them from the objects they orbited.<sup>[*[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia_citation_needed)*]</sup>

The first to use the term *satellite* to describe orbiting bodies was the German astronomer [Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler) in his pamphlet *Narratio de Observatis a se quatuor Iouis satellitibus erronibus* ("Narration About Four Satellites of Jupiter Observed") in 1610. He derived the term from the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin) word *satelles*, meaning "guard", "attendant", or "companion", because the *satellites* accompanied their primary planet in their journey through the heavens.

The term *satellite* thus became the normal one for referring to an object orbiting a planet, as it avoided the ambiguity of "moon". In 1957, however, the launching of the artificial object [Sputnik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sputnik) created a need for new terminology. The terms *man-made satellite* and *artificial moon* were very quickly abandoned in favor of the simpler *satellite*, and as a consequence, the term has become linked primarily with artificial objects flown in space.<sup>[*[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia_citation_needed)*]</sup>

Because of this shift in meaning, the term *moon*, which had continued to be used in a generic sense in works of popular science and fiction, has regained respectability and is now used interchangeably with *natural satellite*, even in scientific articles. When it is necessary to avoid both the ambiguity of confusion with Earth's natural satellite the Moon and the natural satellites of the other planets on the one hand, and artificial satellites on the other, the term *natural satellite* (using "natural" in a sense opposed to "artificial") is used. To further avoid ambiguity, the convention is to capitalize the word Moon when referring to Earth's natural satellite (a [proper noun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_noun), but not when referring to other natural satellites ([common nouns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_noun).

Many authors define "satellite" or "natural satellite" as orbiting some planet or minor planet, synonymous with "moon" – by such a definition all natural satellites are moons, but Earth and other planets are not satellites. A few recent authors define "moon" as "a satellite of a planet or minor planet", and "planet" as "a satellite of a star" – such authors consider Earth as a "natural satellite of the Sun".

### Definition of a moon
*Size comparison of [Earth](/celestial-objects/planets/subearths/earth/) and the [Moon](/celestial-objects/moons/moon/).*
There is no established lower limit on what is considered a "moon". Every natural celestial body with an identified orbit around a planet of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System), some as small as a kilometer across, has been considered a moon, though objects a tenth that size within Saturn's rings, which have not been directly observed, have been called *[moonlets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlet)*. Small [asteroid moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor_planet_(moon)) (natural satellites of asteroids), such as [Dactyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dactyl_(moon)), have also been called moonlets.

The upper limit is also vague. Two orbiting bodies are sometimes described as a [double planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_(planet)) rather than primary and satellite. [Asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroids) such as [90 Antiope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/90_Antiope) are considered double asteroids, but they have not forced a clear definition of what constitutes a moon. Some authors consider the Pluto–Charon system to be a double (dwarf) planet. The most common<sup>[*[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia_citation_needed)*]</sup> dividing line on what is considered a moon rests upon whether the [barycentre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycentre) is below the surface of the larger body, though this is somewhat arbitrary because it depends on distance as well as relative mass.

## Origin and orbital characteristics
Further information: [Circumplanetary disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumplanetary_disk)

The natural satellites orbiting relatively close to the planet on [prograde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retrograde_and_prograde_motion), uninclined circular orbits ([*regular* satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_(moon)) are generally thought to have been formed out of the same [collapsing region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumplanetary_disk) of the [protoplanetary disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk) that created its primary. In contrast, [irregular satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irregular_(satellite)) (generally orbiting on distant, [inclined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_inclination), [eccentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity) and/or [retrograde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retrograde_motion) orbits) are thought to be captured [asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid) possibly further fragmented by collisions. Most of the major natural satellites of the Solar System have regular orbits, while most of the small natural satellites have irregular orbits. The [Moon](/celestial-objects/moons/moon/) and the [Moons of Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons_of_Pluto) are exceptions among large bodies in that they are thought to have originated from the collision of two large protoplanetary objects early in the Solar System's history (see the [giant impact hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis). The material that would have been placed in orbit around the central body is predicted to have reaccreted to form one or more orbiting natural satellites. As opposed to planetary-sized bodies, [asteroid moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_(moon)) are thought to commonly form by this process. [Triton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triton_(moon)) is another exception; although large and in a close, circular orbit, its motion is retrograde and it is thought to be a captured [dwarf planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_(planet)).

### Temporary satellites
Main article: [Temporary satellite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temporary_satellite_(satellite))

The capture of an asteroid from a heliocentric orbit is not always permanent. According to simulations, [temporary satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temporary_(satellite)) should be a common phenomenon. The only observed examples are [1991 VG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1991_VG), [2006 RH120](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006_RH120), [2020 CD3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_CD3).

[2006 RH120](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006_RH120) was a temporary satellite of Earth for nine months in 2006 and 2007.

## Tidal locking
Main article: [Tidal locking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_locking)

Most [regular moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_(moon)) (natural satellites following relatively close and prograde orbits with small orbital inclination and eccentricity) in the Solar System are tidally locked to their respective primaries, meaning that the same side of the natural satellite always faces its planet. This phenomenon comes about through a loss of energy due to tidal forces raised by the planet, slowing the rotation of the satellite until it is negligible. Exceptions are known; one such exception is [Saturn](/celestial-objects/planets/gas-giants/saturn/)'s natural satellite [Hyperion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperion_(moon)), which rotates chaotically because of the gravitational influence of [Titan](/celestial-objects/moons/titan-moon/). Pluto's four, circumbinary small moons also rotate chaotically due to Charon's influence.

In contrast, the outer natural satellites of the giant planets (irregular satellites) are too far away to have become locked. For example, Jupiter's [Himalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Himalia_(moon)), Saturn's [Phoebe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoebe_(moon)), and Neptune's [Nereid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nereid_(moon)) have rotation periods in the range of ten hours, whereas their orbital periods are hundreds of days.

## Satellites of satellites
Main article: [Subsatellite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subsatellite)
impression of [Rhea's proposed rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhea%27s_proposed_rings_(moon))
No "moons of moons" or subsatellites (natural satellites that orbit a natural satellite of a planet) are currently known. In most cases, the tidal effects of the planet would make such a system unstable.

However, calculations performed after the 2008 detection of a possible [ring system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rings_of_rhea) around Saturn's moon [Rhea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhea_(moon)) indicate that satellites orbiting Rhea could have stable orbits. Furthermore, the suspected rings are thought to be narrow, a phenomenon normally associated with [shepherd moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shepherd_(moon)). However, targeted images taken by the *[Cassini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini)* spacecraft failed to detect rings around Rhea.

It has also been proposed that Saturn's moon [Iapetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iapetus_(moon)) had a satellite in the past; this is one of several hypotheses that have been put forward to account for its [equatorial ridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equatorial_ridge_on_iapetus).

Light-curve analysis suggests that Saturn's irregular satellite [Kiviuq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kiviuq_(moon)) is extremely prolate, and is likely a [contact binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contact_binary_small_solar_system_body) or even a binary moon.

## Trojan satellites
Two natural satellites are known to have small companions at both their [L<sub>4</sub>](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point_l4_20and_20l5) and [L<sub>5</sub>](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point_l4_20and_20l5) [Lagrangian points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_points), sixty degrees ahead and behind the body in its orbit. These companions are called [trojan moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trojan_(moon)), as their orbits are analogous to the [trojan asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trojan_(asteroid)) of [Jupiter](/celestial-objects/planets/gas-giants/jupiter/). The trojan moons are [Telesto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telesto_(moon)) and [Calypso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calypso_(moon)), which are the leading and following companions, respectively, of the Saturnian moon [Tethys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tethys_(moon)); and [Helene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helene_(moon)) and [Polydeuces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polydeuces_(moon)), the leading and following companions of the Saturnian moon [Dione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dione_(moon)).

## Asteroid satellites
Main article: [Asteroid moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_moon_(moon))

The discovery of [243 Ida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/243_Ida)'s natural satellite [Dactyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dactyl_(moon)) in the early 1990s confirmed that some [asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid) have natural satellites; indeed, [87 Sylvia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/87_Sylvia) has two. Some, such as [90 Antiope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/90_Antiope), are double asteroids with two comparably sized components.

## Shape
See also: [Satellite planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_planet_(planet))
*Relative masses of the natural satellites of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System). [Mimas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mimas_(moon)), [Enceladus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enceladus), and [Miranda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miranda_(moon)) are too small to be visible at this scale. All the irregularly shaped natural satellites, even added together, would also be too small to be visible.*
Neptune's moon [Proteus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proteus_(moon)) is the largest irregularly shaped natural satellite; the shapes of Eris' moon [Dysnomia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysnomia_(moon)) and [Orcus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orcus)' moon [Vanth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanth_(moon)) are unknown. All other known natural satellites that are at least the size of Uranus's [Miranda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miranda_(moon)) have lapsed into rounded [ellipsoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid) under [hydrostatic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium), i.e. are "round/rounded satellites" and are sometimes categorized as [planetary-mass moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_mass_(moon)). (Dysnomia's density is known to be high enough that it is probably a solid ellipsoid as well.) The larger natural satellites, being tidally locked, tend toward [ovoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oval) (egg-like) shapes: squat at their poles and with longer equatorial axes in the direction of their primaries (their planets) than in the direction of their motion. Saturn's moon [Mimas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mimas_(moon)), for example, has a major axis 9% greater than its polar axis and 5% greater than its other equatorial axis. [Methone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methone_(moon)), another of Saturn's moons, is only around 3 km in diameter and visibly egg-shaped. The effect is smaller on the largest natural satellites, where their gravity is greater relative to the effects of tidal distortion, especially those that orbit less massive planets or, as in the case of the Moon, at greater distances.

## Geological activity
Of the twenty known natural satellites in the Solar System that are large enough to be gravitationally rounded, several remain geologically active today. [Io](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Io_(moon)) is the most volcanically active body in the Solar System, while [Europa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa_(moon)), [Enceladus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enceladus), [Titan](/celestial-objects/moons/titan-moon/) and [Triton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triton_(moon)) display evidence of ongoing [tectonic activity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tectonics) and [cryovolcanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryovolcano). In the first three cases, the geological activity is powered by the [tidal heating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_heating_tidal_20heating) resulting from having [eccentric orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity) close to their giant-planet primaries. (This mechanism would have also operated on Triton in the past before its orbit was [circularized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_circularization).) Many other natural satellites, such as Earth's Moon, [Ganymede](/celestial-objects/moons/ganymede-moon/), Tethys, and Miranda, show evidence of past geological activity, resulting from energy sources such as the [decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay) of their [primordial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primordial_nuclide) [radioisotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radionuclide), greater past orbital eccentricities (due in some cases to past [orbital resonances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_resonance), or the [differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_differentiation) or freezing of their interiors. Enceladus and Triton both have active features resembling [geysers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geysers), although in the case of Triton solar heating appears to provide the energy. Titan and Triton have significant atmospheres; Titan also has [hydrocarbon lakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lakes_of_titan). All four of the Galilean moons have atmospheres, though they are extremely thin. Four of the largest natural satellites, Europa, Ganymede, [Callisto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Callisto_(moon)), and Titan, are thought to have subsurface oceans of liquid water, while smaller Enceladus also supports a global subsurface ocean of liquid water.

## Occurrence in the Solar System
Largest moons to scale with their parent planets and dwarf planet.
Besides planets and dwarf planets objects within our Solar System known to have natural satellites are 76 in the [asteroid belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_belt) (five with two each), four [Jupiter trojans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter_trojans), 39 [near-Earth objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-Earth_objects) (two with two satellites each), and 14 [Mars-crossers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars-crossers). There are also 84 known natural satellites of [trans-Neptunian objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_objects). Some 150 additional small bodies have been observed within the [rings of Saturn](/uncategorized/rings-of-saturn/), but only a few were tracked long enough to establish orbits. Planets around other stars are likely to have satellites as well, and although numerous candidates have been detected to date, none have yet been confirmed.

Of the inner planets, [Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(planet)) and [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus) have no natural satellites; Earth has one large natural satellite, known as the Moon; and [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars) has two tiny natural satellites, [Phobos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phobos_(moon)) and [Deimos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deimos_(moon)). The [giant planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_(planet)) have extensive systems of natural satellites, including half a dozen comparable in size to Earth's Moon: the four [Galilean moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_moons_(moon)), Saturn's Titan, and [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune)'s Triton. Saturn has an additional six mid-sized natural satellites massive enough to have achieved [hydrostatic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium), and [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus) has five. It has been suggested that some [satellites may potentially harbour life](/celestial-objects/moons/natural-satellite-habitability/).

Among the objects generally agreed by astronomers to be dwarf planets, [Ceres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceres_(planet)) and [Sedna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedna_(planet)) have no known natural satellites. Pluto has the relatively large natural satellite Charon and four smaller natural satellites; [Styx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Styx_(moon)), [Nix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nix_(moon)), [Kerberos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kerberos_(moon)), and [Hydra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydra_(moon)). [Haumea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haumea) has two natural satellites; [Orcus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orcus), [Quaoar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaoar), [Makemake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Makemake), [Gonggong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gonggong_(planet)), and [Eris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eris_(planet)) have one each. The Pluto–Charon system is unusual in that the [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass_barycenter_20in_20astrophysics_20and_20astronomy) lies in open space between the two, a characteristic sometimes associated with a [double-planet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_(planet)) system.

The seven largest [natural satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_natural_satellites) in the Solar System (those bigger than 2,500 km across) are Jupiter's [Galilean moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_moons_(moon)) (Ganymede, [Callisto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Callisto_(moon)), Io, and [Europa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa_(moon)))), Saturn's moon Titan, Earth's moon, and Neptune's captured natural satellite Triton. Triton, the smallest of these, has more mass than all smaller natural satellites together. Similarly in the next size group of nine mid-sized natural satellites, between 1,000 km and 1,600 km across, [Titania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titania_(moon)), [Oberon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oberon_(moon)), [Rhea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhea_(moon)), [Iapetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iapetus_(moon)), Charon, [Ariel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ariel_(moon)), [Umbriel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umbriel_(moon)), [Dione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dione_(moon)), and Tethys, the smallest, Tethys, has more mass than all smaller natural satellites together. As well as the natural satellites of the various planets, there are also over 80 known natural satellites of the [dwarf planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_(planet)), [minor planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor_(planet)) and other [small Solar System bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_Solar_System_bodies). Some studies estimate that up to 15% of all trans-Neptunian objects could have satellites.
