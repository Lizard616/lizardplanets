In [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy), **axial tilt**, also known as **obliquity**, is the [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle) between an object's [rotational axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis) and its [orbital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit) axis, which is the line [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular) to its [orbital plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_plane_astronomy); equivalently, it is the angle between its [equatorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator) plane and orbital plane. It differs from [orbital inclination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_inclination).

At an obliquity of 0 degrees, the two axes point in the same direction; that is, the rotational axis is perpendicular to the orbital plane.

The rotational axis of [Earth](/celestial-objects/planets/subearths/earth/), for example, is the imaginary line that passes through both the [North Pole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Pole) and [South Pole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Pole), whereas the Earth's orbital axis is the line perpendicular to the imaginary [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_geometry) through which the Earth moves as it revolves around the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun); the Earth's obliquity or axial tilt is the angle between these two lines.

Over the course of an [orbital period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_period), the obliquity usually does not change considerably, and the [orientation of the axis remains the same](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axial_parallelism) relative to the [background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_sphere) of [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed_stars). This causes one pole to be pointed more toward the Sun on one side of the orbit, and more away from the Sun on the other side—the cause of the [seasons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Season) on Earth.

## Standards
There are two standard methods of specifying a planet's tilt. One way is based on the planet's *north pole*, defined in relation to the direction of Earth's north pole, and the other way is based on the planet's *positive pole*, defined by the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_angle_representation):

- The [International Astronomical Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union) (IAU) defines the *north pole* of a planet as that which lies on Earth's north side of the [invariable plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariable_plane) of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System); under this system, [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus) is tilted 3° and rotates [retrograde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retrograde_motion), opposite that of most of the other planets.
- The IAU also uses the right-hand rule to define a *positive pole* for the purpose of determining orientation. Using this convention, Venus is tilted 177° ("upside down") and rotates prograde.

## Earth
Further information: [Ecliptic § Obliquity of the ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic_§_Obliquity_of_the_ecliptic)

See also: [Earth's rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation) and [Earth-centered inertial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth-centered_inertial)

[Earth](/celestial-objects/planets/subearths/earth/)'s [orbital plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit) is known as the [ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic) plane, and [Earth's tilt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_tilt) is known to astronomers as the *obliquity of the ecliptic*, being the angle between the ecliptic and the [celestial equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_equator) on the [celestial sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_sphere). It is denoted by the [Greek letter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_letter) *[ε](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epsilon)*.

Earth currently has an axial tilt of about 23.44°. This value remains about the same relative to a stationary orbital plane throughout the cycles of [axial precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axial_precession). But the ecliptic (i.e., Earth's orbit) moves due to planetary [perturbations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_astronomy), and the obliquity of the ecliptic is not a fixed quantity. At present, it is decreasing at a rate of about [46.8″](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_of_arc) per [century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Century) *(see details in [Short term](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short_term) below)*.

### History
The ancient Greeks had good measurements of the obliquity since about 350 BCE, when [Pytheas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pytheas) of Marseilles measured the shadow of a [gnomon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gnomon) at the summer solstice. About 830 CE, the Caliph [Al-Mamun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Mamun) of Baghdad directed his astronomers to measure the obliquity, and the result was used in the Arab world for many years. In 1437, [Ulugh Beg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulugh_Beg) determined the Earth's axial tilt as 23°30'17″ (23.5047°).

During the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages), it was widely believed that both precession and Earth's obliquity oscillated around a mean value, with a period of 672 years, an idea known as *[trepidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trepidation_astronomy))* of the equinoxes. Perhaps the first to realize this was incorrect (during historic time) was [Ibn al-Shatir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_al-Shatir) in the fourteenth century and the first to realize that the obliquity is decreasing at a relatively constant rate was [Fracastoro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fracastoro) in 1538. The first accurate, modern, western observations of the obliquity were probably those of [Tycho Brahe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tycho_Brahe) from [Denmark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denmark), about 1584, although observations by several others, including [al-Ma'mun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Ma%27mun), [al-Tusi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Tusi), [Purbach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purbach), [Regiomontanus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regiomontanus), and [Walther](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walther), could have provided similar information.

### Oscillation
#### Short term
of the ecliptic for 20,000 years, from [Laskar (1986). The red point represents the year 2000.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_laskar)
The exact angular value of the obliquity is found by observation of the motions of Earth and [planets](/celestial-objects/planets/) over many years. Astronomers produce new [fundamental ephemerides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_ephemeris) as the accuracy of [observation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observational_astronomy) improves and as the understanding of the [dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_dynamics) increases, and from these ephemerides various astronomical values, including the obliquity, are derived.

Annual [almanacs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almanac) are published listing the derived values and methods of use. Until 1983, the [Astronomical Almanac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_Almanac)'s angular value of the mean obliquity for any date was calculated based on the [work of Newcomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_of_Newcomb), who analyzed positions of the planets until about 1895:

: *ε* = 23°27'8.26″ − 46.845″ *T* − 0.0059″ *T*<sup>2</sup> + 0.00181″ *T*<sup>3</sup>

where *ε* is the obliquity and *T* is [tropical centuries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_year) from [B1900.0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B1900.0)#Besselian%20years) to the date in question.

From 1984, the [Jet Propulsion Laboratory's DE series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory%27s_DE_series) of computer-generated ephemerides took over as the [fundamental ephemeris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_ephemeris) of the [Astronomical Almanac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_Almanac). Obliquity based on DE200, which analyzed observations from 1911 to 1979, was calculated:

: *ε* = 23°26'21.448″ − 46.8150″ *T* − 0.00059″ *T*<sup>2</sup> + 0.001813″ *T*<sup>3</sup>

where hereafter *T* is [Julian centuries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_centuries) from [J2000.0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J2000.0)#Julian%20years%20and%20J2000).

JPL's fundamental ephemerides have been continually updated. For instance, according to IAU resolution in 2006 in favor of the P03 astronomical model, the *Astronomical Almanac* for 2010 specifies:

: *ε* = 23°26'21.406″ − 46.836769″ *T* − 0.0001831″ *T*<sup>2</sup> + 0.00200340″ *T*<sup>3</sup> − 5.76″ × 10<sup>−7</sup> *T*<sup>4</sup> − 4.34″ × 10<sup>−8</sup> *T*<sup>5</sup>

These expressions for the obliquity are intended for high precision over a relatively short time span, perhaps ± several centuries. [Jacques Laskar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Laskar) computed an expression to order *T*<sup>10</sup> good to 0.02″ over 1000 years and several [arcseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minute_of_arc) over 10,000 years.

: *ε* = 23°26'21.448″ − 4680.93″ *t* − 1.55″ *t*<sup>2</sup> + 1999.25″ *t*<sup>3</sup> − 51.38″ *t*<sup>4</sup> − 249.67″ *t*<sup>5</sup> − 39.05″ *t*<sup>6</sup> + 7.12″ *t*<sup>7</sup> + 27.87″ *t*<sup>8</sup> + 5.79″ *t*<sup>9</sup> + 2.45″ *t*<sup>10</sup>

where here *t* is multiples of 10,000 [Julian years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_years) from [J2000.0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J2000.0)#Julian%20years%20and%20J2000).

These expressions are for the so-called *mean* obliquity, that is, the obliquity free from short-term variations. Periodic motions of the Moon and of Earth in its orbit cause much smaller (9.2 [arcseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minute_of_arc) short-period (about 18.6 years) oscillations of the rotation axis of Earth, known as [nutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_nutation), which add a periodic component to Earth's obliquity. The *true* or instantaneous obliquity includes this nutation.

#### Long term
Main article: [Formation and evolution of the Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System)

Main article: [Milankovitch cycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles)

Using [numerical methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_methods) to simulate [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System) behavior over a period of several million years, long-term changes in Earth's [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit), and hence its obliquity, have been investigated. For the past 5 million years, Earth's obliquity has varied between 22°2'33″ and 24°30'16″, with a mean period of 41,040 years. This cycle is a combination of precession and the largest [term](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addend) in the motion of the [ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic). For the next 1 million years, the cycle will carry the obliquity between 22°13'44″ and 24°20'50″.

The [Moon](/celestial-objects/moons/moon/) has a stabilizing effect on Earth's obliquity. Frequency map analysis conducted in 1993 suggested that, in the absence of the Moon, the obliquity could change rapidly due to [orbital resonances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_resonance) and [chaotic behavior of the Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaotic_behavior_of_the_Solar_System), reaching as high as 90° in as little as a few million years (*also see [Orbit of the Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon_(moon))*). However, more recent numerical simulations made in 2011 indicated that even in the absence of the Moon, Earth's obliquity might not be quite so unstable; varying only by about 20–25°. To resolve this contradiction, diffusion rate of obliquity has been calculated, and it was found that it takes more than billions of years for Earth's obliquity to reach near 90°. The Moon's stabilizing effect will continue for less than two billion years. As the Moon continues to recede from Earth due to [tidal acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_acceleration), resonances may occur which will cause large oscillations of the obliquity.

## Solar System bodies
See also: [Poles of astronomical bodies § Poles of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poles_of_astronomical_bodies_§_Poles_of_rotation)
[thumb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File_planets_and_dwarf_planets_27_tilt_and_rotation_speed_webm)
All four of the innermost, rocky planets of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System) may have had large variations of their obliquity in the past. Since obliquity is the angle between the axis of rotation and the direction perpendicular to the orbital plane, it changes as the orbital plane changes due to the influence of other planets. But the axis of rotation can also move ([axial precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axial_precession), due to torque exerted by the Sun on a planet's equatorial bulge. Like Earth, all of the rocky planets show axial precession. If the precession rate were very fast the obliquity would actually remain fairly constant even as the orbital plane changes. The rate varies due to [tidal dissipation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_acceleration) and [core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_core)-[mantle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mantle_geology) interaction, among other things. When a planet's precession rate approaches certain values, [orbital resonances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_resonance) may cause large changes in obliquity. The amplitude of the contribution having one of the resonant rates is divided by the difference between the resonant rate and the precession rate, so it becomes large when the two are similar.

[Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(planet)) and [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus) have most likely been stabilized by the tidal dissipation of the Sun. Earth was stabilized by the Moon, as mentioned above, but before its [formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon_formation), Earth, too, could have passed through times of instability. [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars)'s obliquity is quite variable over millions of years and may be in a chaotic state; it varies as much as 0° to 60° over some millions of years, depending on [perturbations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_astronomy) of the planets. Some authors dispute that Mars's obliquity is chaotic, and show that tidal dissipation and viscous core-mantle coupling are adequate for it to have reached a fully damped state, similar to Mercury and Venus.

The occasional shifts in the axial tilt of Mars have been suggested as an explanation for the appearance and disappearance of rivers and lakes over the course of the existence of Mars. A shift could cause a burst of methane into the atmosphere, causing warming, but then the methane would be destroyed and the climate would become arid again.

The obliquities of the outer planets are considered relatively stable.

## Extrasolar planets
The stellar obliquity *ψ*<sub>s</sub>, i.e. the axial tilt of a star with respect to the orbital plane of one of its planets, has been determined for only a few systems. By 2012, 49 stars have had sky-projected spin-orbit misalignment *λ* has been observed, which serves as a lower limit to *ψ*<sub>s</sub>. Most of these measurements rely on the [Rossiter–McLaughlin effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rossiter–McLaughlin_effect). Since the launch of space-based telescopes such as [Kepler space telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler_space_telescope), it has been made possible to determine and estimate the obliquity of an extrasolar planet. The rotational flattening of the planet and the entourage of moons and/or rings, which are traceable with high-precision photometry provide access to planetary obliquity, *ψ*<sub>p</sub>. Many extrasolar planets have since had their obliquity determined, such as [Kepler-186f](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-186f) and [Kepler-413b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler-413b).

Astrophysicists have applied tidal theories to predict the obliquity of [extrasolar planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extrasolar_planets). It has been shown that the obliquities of exoplanets in the [habitable zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitable_zone) around low-mass stars tend to be eroded in less than 10<sup>9</sup> years, which means that they would not have tilt-induced seasons as Earth has.
