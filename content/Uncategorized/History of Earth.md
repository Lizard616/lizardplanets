## Natural history
Main articles: [History of Earth](/uncategorized/history-of-earth/) and [Timeline of natural history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_natural_history)

### Formation
Further information: [Early Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Earth) and [Hadean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadean)
*2012 artistic impression of the early [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System)'s [protoplanetary disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk) from which Earth and other Solar System bodies were formed.*
The oldest material found in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System) is dated to 4.5682+0.0002

−0.0004 [Ga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ga) (billion years) ago. By 4.54±0.04 Ga the primordial Earth had formed. The bodies in [the Solar System formed and evolved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Solar_System_formed_and_evolved) with the Sun. In theory, a [solar nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_(nebula)) partitions a volume out of a [molecular cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_cloud) by gravitational collapse, which begins to spin and flatten into a [circumstellar disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumstellar_disk), and then the planets grow out of that disk with the Sun. A nebula contains gas, ice grains, and [dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust) (including [primordial nuclides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primordial_nuclide). According to [nebular theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebular_theory), [planetesimals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetesimal) formed by [accretion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_astrophysics), with the primordial Earth being estimated as likely taking anywhere from 70 to 100 million years to form.

Estimates of the age of the Moon range from 4.5 Ga to significantly younger. A [leading hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis) is that it was formed by accretion from material loosed from Earth after a [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars)-sized object with about 10% of Earth's mass, named [Theia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theia_(planet)), collided with Earth. It hit Earth with a glancing blow and some of its mass merged with Earth. Between approximately 4.1 and 3.8 Ga, numerous [asteroid impacts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impact_event) during the [Late Heavy Bombardment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Heavy_Bombardment) caused significant changes to the greater surface environment of the Moon and, by inference, to that of Earth.

### After formation
Main article: [Geological history of Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geological_history_of_Earth)

[Earth's atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere) and oceans were formed by [volcanic activity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanism) and [outgassing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outgassing). Water vapor from these sources [condensed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_water_on_earth) into the oceans, augmented by water and ice from asteroids, [protoplanets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoplanet), and [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet). Sufficient water to fill the oceans may have been on Earth since it formed. In this model, atmospheric [greenhouse gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas) kept the oceans from freezing when the newly forming Sun [had only 70%](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faint_young_sun_paradox) of its [current luminosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_luminosity). By 3.5 Ga, [Earth's magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field) was established, which helped prevent the atmosphere from being stripped away by the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind).
*Pale orange dot — an artist's impression of [Early Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Earth), featuring its tinted orange [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane)-rich [early atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prebiotic_atmosphere).*
As the molten outer layer of Earth cooled it [formed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_transition) the first solid [crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_crust), which is thought to have been [mafic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mafic) in composition. The first [continental crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_crust), which was more [felsic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felsic) in composition, formed by the partial melting of this mafic crust. The presence of grains of the [mineral zircon of Hadean age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mineral_zircon_of_Hadean_age) in [Eoarchean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eoarchean) [sedimentary rocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedimentary_rock) suggests that at least some felsic crust existed as early as 4.4 Ga, only 140 [Ma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ma) after Earth's formation. There are two main models of how this initial small volume of continental crust evolved to reach its current abundance: (1) a relatively steady growth up to the present day, which is supported by the radiometric dating of continental crust globally and (2) an initial rapid growth in the volume of continental crust during the [Archean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archean), forming the bulk of the continental crust that now exists, which is supported by isotopic evidence from [hafnium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hafnium) in [zircons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zircon) and [neodymium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neodymium) in sedimentary rocks. The two models and the data that support them can be reconciled by large-scale [recycling of the continental crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crustal_recycling), particularly during the early stages of Earth's history.

New continental crust forms as a result of [plate tectonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plate_tectonics), a process ultimately driven by the continuous loss of heat from Earth's interior. Over [the period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologic_time_scale) of hundreds of millions of years, tectonic forces have caused areas of continental crust to group together to form [supercontinents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercontinent) that have subsequently broken apart. At approximately 750 Ma, one of the earliest known supercontinents, [Rodinia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodinia), began to break apart. The continents later recombined to form [Pannotia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pannotia) at 600–540 Ma, then finally [Pangaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pangaea), which also began to break apart at 180 Ma.

The most recent pattern of [ice ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_age) began about 40 Ma, and then intensified during the [Pleistocene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pleistocene) about 3 Ma. [High-](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-) and [middle-latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_latitude) regions have since undergone repeated cycles of glaciation and thaw, repeating about every 21,000, 41,000 and 100,000 years. The [Last Glacial Period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Last_Glacial_Period), colloquially called the "last ice age", covered large parts of the continents, to the middle latitudes, in ice and ended about 11,700 years ago.

### Origin of life and evolution
Main articles: [Origin of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_life), [Earliest known life forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms), and [History of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_life)

[Chemical reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reactions) led to the first self-replicating molecules about four billion years ago. A half billion years later, the [last common ancestor of all current life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Last_universal_common_ancestor) arose. The evolution of [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis) allowed the Sun's energy to be harvested directly by life forms. The resultant [molecular oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_oxygen) (O2) accumulated in the atmosphere and due to interaction with ultraviolet solar radiation, formed a protective [ozone layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_layer) (O3) in the upper atmosphere. The incorporation of smaller cells within larger ones resulted in the [development of complex cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endosymbiotic_theory) called [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote). True multicellular organisms formed as cells within [colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colony_biology) became increasingly specialized. Aided by the absorption of harmful [ultraviolet radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet_radiation) by the ozone layer, life colonized Earth's surface. Among the earliest [fossil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil) evidence for life is [microbial mat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbial_mat) fossils found in 3.48 billion-year-old [sandstone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandstone) in [Western Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Australia), [biogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogenic_substance) [graphite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphite) found in 3.7 billion-year-old [metasedimentary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasediment) rocks in [Western Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Greenland), and remains of [biotic material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biotic_material) found in 4.1 billion-year-old rocks in Western Australia. The [earliest direct evidence of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms) on Earth is contained in 3.45 billion-year-old [Australian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australian) rocks showing fossils of [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism).
*An artist's impression of the [Archean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archean), the [eon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologic_time_scale) after Earth's formation, featuring round [stromatolites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stromatolite), which are early oxygen-producing forms of life from billions of years ago. After the [Late Heavy Bombardment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Heavy_Bombardment), [Earth's crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_crust) had cooled, its water-rich barren [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_surface) is marked by [continents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent) and [volcanoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcano), with the Moon still orbiting Earth half as far as it is today, appearing 2.8 times larger and producing strong [tides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tide).*
During the [Neoproterozoic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoproterozoic), 1000 to 539 Ma, much of Earth might have been covered in ice. This hypothesis has been termed "[Snowball Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snowball_Earth)", and it is of particular interest because it preceded the [Cambrian explosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambrian_explosion), when multicellular life forms significantly increased in complexity. Following the Cambrian explosion, 535 Ma, there have been at least five major [mass extinctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_event) and many minor ones. Apart from the proposed current [Holocene extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holocene_extinction) event, the [most recent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cretaceous_paleogene_extinction_event) was 66 Ma, when [an asteroid impact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicxulub_impactor) triggered the extinction of non-avian dinosaurs and other large reptiles, but largely spared small animals such as insects, [mammals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mammal), lizards and birds. Mammalian life has diversified over the past 66 Mys, and several million years ago, an African [ape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ape) species gained the ability to stand upright. This facilitated tool use and encouraged communication that provided the nutrition and stimulation needed for a larger brain, which led to the [evolution of humans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_evolution). The [development of agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_agriculture), and then [civilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_ancient_civilizations), led to humans having an [influence on Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Influence_on_Earth) and the nature and quantity of other life forms that continues to this day.

### Future
Main article: [Future of Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Future_of_Earth)

See also: [Global catastrophic risk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_catastrophic_risk)
*Conjectured illustration of the scorched Earth after the Sun has entered the [red giant](/uncategorized/red-giant/) phase, about 5–7 billion years from now.*
Earth's expected long-term future is tied to that of the Sun. Over the next 1.1 billion years, solar luminosity will increase by 10%, and over the next 3.5 billion years by 40%. Earth's increasing surface temperature will accelerate the [inorganic carbon cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonate_silicate_cycle), reducing CO2 concentration to levels lethally low for plants (10 [ppm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_million) for [C4 photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C4_photosynthesis) in approximately 100–900 million years. The lack of vegetation will result in the loss of oxygen in the atmosphere, making animal life impossible. Due to the increased luminosity, Earth's mean temperature may reach 100 °C (212 °F) in 1.5 billion years, and all ocean water will evaporate and be lost to space, which may trigger a [runaway greenhouse effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Runaway_greenhouse_effect), within an estimated 1.6 to 3 billion years. Even if the Sun were stable, a fraction of the water in the modern oceans will descend to the [mantle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mantle_geology), due to reduced steam venting from mid-ocean ridges.

The Sun will [evolve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution) to become a [red giant](/uncategorized/red-giant/) in about 5 billion years. Models predict that the Sun will expand to roughly 1 [AU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AU) (150 million km; 93 million mi), about 250 times its present radius. Earth's fate is less clear. As a red giant, the Sun will lose roughly 30% of its mass, so, without tidal effects, Earth will move to an orbit 1.7 AU (250 million km; 160 million mi) from the Sun when the star reaches its maximum radius, otherwise, with tidal effects, it may enter the Sun's atmosphere and be vaporized.
